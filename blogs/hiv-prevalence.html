Predicting HIV Prevalence in Kenya: A Logistic Regression Analysis

Machine Learning · Public Health · Logistic Regression · Kenya (2000–2023)

Project Overview

This project investigates the relationship between health system strength, financing mechanisms, and socioeconomic indicators in shaping HIV prevalence trends in Kenya from 2000 to 2023.

Research Question: Which socioeconomic and health system factors are the strongest predictors of high HIV prevalence in Kenya?

Key Finding: Universal Health Coverage (UHC) for noncommunicable diseases, health expenditure per capita, and the share of private health spending jointly achieved 100% classification accuracy.

Dataset Description

  • Source: WHO Global Health Observatory
  • Period: 2000–2023 (24 years)
  • Total indicators: 49
  • Target variable: HIV prevalence (High > 5.25%, Low ≤ 5.25%)

Methodology

Data Processing

  • Integrated six WHO datasets
  • Reshaped data to yearly wide format
  • Merged datasets on year
  • Handled missing values via thresholding and median imputation
  • Removed highly collinear predictors

Model Development

  • Algorithm: Logistic Regression (L2 regularization)
  • Feature scaling using StandardScaler
  • Train/Test split: 70/30 with stratification
  • Evaluation: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, ROC AUC

Key Results

The final model selected three representative predictors: UHC NCD coverage, private health expenditure share, and health expenditure per capita.

Model Accuracy: 100%

ROC AUC: 1.00

Interpretation: Strong health systems and higher public health investment are associated with lower HIV prevalence.

Policy Implications

  • Strengthen UHC coverage, especially for NCD services
  • Increase public health spending per capita
  • Reduce reliance on private and out-of-pocket health financing
  • Use financing indicators as early warning signals

Limitations

  • Small sample size (24 observations)
  • National-level aggregation masks regional variation
  • Wide confidence intervals indicate uncertainty in effect sizes
  • Causality cannot be inferred

Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that HIV prevalence is closely tied to broader health system performance. Investments in universal coverage and public health financing play a critical role in long-term disease control.
